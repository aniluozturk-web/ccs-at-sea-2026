  • Simone Mamini City St George's, University of London, United Kingdom Governing CO₂ in Shipping: Strengthening the IMO-UNCLOS Framework in Light of the ITLOS Advisory Opinion on Climate Change and International Law

    Drawing on the recent ITLOS Advisory Opinion on Climate Change, this presentation argues that economic instruments within the IMO GHG Strategy alone cannot support the CCS sector, and that a unified legal framework integrating ITLOS-informed obligations into the IMO regulatory process is the most credible pathway for governing CO₂ shipping and maritime decarbonisation.

  • Levent Bilgili Bursa Technical University, Turkey Transboundary CO₂ Shipping and CCS: Legal and Environmental Challenges in a Well-to-Wake Context

    This paper argues that integrating well-to-wake life cycle assessment, as reflected in the IMO Life Cycle GHG Guidelines (MEPC.391(81)), into permitting and environmental impact procedures is essential to avoid burden-shifting across jurisdictions and to ensure that transboundary CO₂ shipping develops as a legally robust and environmentally credible component of global CCS governance.

  • Michael J. T. McElhinney University of Bergen, Norway Regulation of Cross-Border Carbon Transport and Storage: Legal Issues in Enabling Multi-State CCS Projects in the North Sea Carbon Basin

    This presentation focuses on how to define transboundary storage in different scenarios and how cross-border governing agreements can be formulated, drawing comparisons with existing unitisation agreements for straddling oil and gas fields in the North Sea and assessing issues of carbon plume migration, pressure propagation, and liability.

  • Luciana Fernandes Coelho Lund University, Sweden Implications of the BBNJ Agreement on Transboundary CO₂ Transport by Ships for Offshore Storage

    This paper evaluates the interplay between the BBNJ Agreement and the framework governing transboundary CO₂ transport, examining how Area-Based Management Tools may impose supplementary safety obligations and how Environmental Impact Assessment requirements apply to CCS activities with potential impacts in areas beyond national jurisdiction.

  • Rekhil Sukesan Global Feeder Shipping LLC, United Arab Emirates Transboundary Maritime Transport of CO₂: Regulatory Vacuum and Legal Fragmentation

    This paper demonstrates how fragmented ratification, uneven domestic implementation, and divergent interpretive practices under the London Protocol continue to generate legal uncertainty for cross-border CO₂ shipping, arguing that existing instruments are ill-equipped to address the distinctive risk profile of liquefied CO₂ regarding cumulative impacts, fault attribution, and insurability.

  • Morgane Querel D-ICE Engineering, France The Importance of the Entry into Force of the HNS Convention to Regulate Liability in the Seaborne Transboundary Transportation of CO₂ and Ensure Environmental Protection

    This presentation analyses why the 1996 HNS Convention provides a more effective liability framework for maritime CO₂ transport incidents than the currently relied-upon LLMC Convention, and examines whether regulatory amendments might facilitate its entry into force without compromising its effectiveness.

  • Ingvild Ombudstvedt IOM Law, Norway International Subject Matter Experts Impact European Legal Framework for CCS Through Technical Standardization

    This presentation highlights how ISO technical standards developed under TC 265 and the new European CEN/TC 474 are increasingly referenced in CCS legal frameworks and permitting procedures globally, illustrating the growing role of technical experts in shaping regulation through standardisation of CO₂ specifications for transport and storage.

  • Henrik von Zweigbergk and Anna Månsson Geological Survey of Sweden (SGU), Sweden Carbon Storage: Work Towards Simplification of Rules

    This presentation reports on Sweden's governmental assignment to identify barriers to carbon storage permitting, concluding that the main challenge is not any single regulation but rather a combination of unclear rules, knowledge gaps, and the absence of an overarching national CCS strategy with a clearly appointed responsible authority.

  • Bára A. Alexandersdóttir Reykjavík University, Iceland Carbon Storage and Cross-Border Transport: Domestic Permitting Challenges in Iceland

    This paper examines Iceland's domestic permitting framework through the lens of the Carbfix Coda Terminal project, illustrating how public concerns about Iceland becoming a repository for foreign CO₂ and intersecting obligations under the London Protocol, OSPAR, and the Aarhus Convention contributed to administrative delays that ultimately halted the project.

  • Lina Lefstad and Felix Schulz LUCSUS, Lund University, Sweden Beyond Market Logic: The Coordinator State and the Governance of CCS Deployment in Europe

    Through participant observation at 18 industry and policy events and 12 semi-structured expert interviews, this paper identifies a misalignment between the CCS value chain's material characteristics and the EU's market-centric governance model, theorising an emergent “coordinator state” logic that moves beyond correcting market failures to actively constructing systemic conditions for deployment.

  • Josep Lluís Díez i Besora Universitat Rovira i Virgili, Spain Adaptability Challenges of Ports for the Viability of Offshore CCS Projects

    This paper analyses how current port concession regimes, typically structured around established cargo flows and short investment cycles, present significant challenges for integrating CCS infrastructure characterised by long development timelines, high capital intensity, and substantial regulatory uncertainty, arguing that ports must evolve into adaptable energy hubs.

  • Romano Alessandro Bove University of Siena, Italy From Operator to State: Rethinking Civil Liability in CCS

    This paper proposes a dual-track liability system: strict operator liability during site selection, injection, and closure phases, followed by a transfer of responsibility to the State as custodian in the post-closure phase, coupled with mandatory insurance coverage to address the potentially severe consequences of CO₂ leakage.

  • Albina Ladynenko Universitat Rovira i Virgili, Spain Challenges for the Insurance Market in the Logistics Chain of Offshore CCS Projects

    This presentation examines the gaps in traditional marine and energy insurance regimes when applied to long-term CO₂ storage, port handling, and maritime transport, considering the adaptations required across construction and installation insurance, civil liability, environmental coverage, and long-term post-operational liability.

  • Patrick Seroogy The George Washington University, United States Balancing Climate Goals and Environmental Risks Through Strict Application of the Precautionary Principle to Carbon Sequestration in the Baltic Sea

    This paper argues that any Baltic Sea CCS project should require strict application of the precautionary principle as codified in the Helsinki Convention, with the Helsinki Commission overseeing a process of cross-boundary scientific consensus that a given operation does not present serious risk of environmental harm before it may proceed.

  • Rosa Manzo University of Bergen, Norway Climate Litigation, Due Diligence Obligations, and Public Interest Concerns in CCS Projects under the Paris Agreement

    This paper examines how recent international and regional court decisions are reshaping legal standards applicable to CCS by embedding public interest considerations, human rights protection, and precautionary reasoning into the assessment of State responsibility, reflecting a shift toward a more substantive conception of climate-related due diligence.

  • Akrita Kaur and Rukhsaar Dhaliwal Panjab University, India Carbon Without a Category: Liquefied CO₂, Maritime Liability, and the Limits of Existing Pollution Regimes

    This paper demonstrates that liquefied CO₂ defies existing regulatory categories under international maritime law — neither conventionally understood as cargo nor as pollution — and examines the legal justification for a sui generis international liability mechanism tailored to the distinctive risk profile of CCS at sea.

  • Ashima Pury and Prapti Chaturvedi Delhi Metropolitan Education, India Recalibrating Maritime Liability for Ship-Borne CCS: From Conventional Pollution Paradigms to Climate Mitigation Infrastructure

    This paper argues that current maritime liability frameworks disproportionately burden ship owners and fail to recognise CO₂ as a climate mitigation substance, advocating for a fundamental shift toward treating ship-borne CO₂ transport as climate infrastructure rather than a residual variant of hazardous cargo carriage.

Day 2

Friday, 8 May 2026

  • Lena Østgaard IOM Law, Norway Lessons from Norway's First-Mover CCS Projects: Regulatory Insights

    This presentation examines how Norway's Sleipner, Snøhvit, and Northern Lights projects have helped regulators and industry identify gaps across the CCS value chain, illustrating that projects can begin before regulatory frameworks are fully mature and that real-world deployment is essential for developing fit-for-purpose regulation.

  • Cody Skahan University of Oxford, United Kingdom After the Pilot: Building Governance and Public Trust in Cross-Border Carbon Storage

    Drawing on anthropological fieldwork conducted in Iceland and abroad, this paper examines how the Carbfix Coda Terminal and the defunct Running Tide project have shaped perceptions of environmental governance in emerging CCS regimes, arguing that positive public attitude is critical for securing social licence and policy continuity.

  • Daopu Somoni University of Aberdeen, United Kingdom Emerging Regulatory Strategies Across the North Atlantic for CCS Technology Deployment: Critical Lessons for Emerging Economies

    This paper examines CCS regulatory strategies in advanced economies including Norway, Canada, Australia, the UK, and the US, identifying approaches such as value chain regulation and a “command-partnership” regulatory model, and draws critical lessons for sustainable deployment in emerging economies like Nigeria and South Africa.

  • Abdulrashid Abdullahi Gombe State University, Nigeria Governing Offshore Carbon Sequestration for Developing Coastal States: Legal and Environmental Implications for Nigeria

    This paper analyses Nigeria's existing legal and policy instruments alongside UNCLOS and the London Protocol, identifying regulatory gaps in permitting, long-term liability, and environmental impact assessment, and proposes governance pathways emphasising precautionary safeguards and harmonisation with international standards for developing coastal states.

  • Clément Lasselin Lund University, Sweden Marine CCS and Basaltic Rocks: Inaccessibility and Promises

    Through cases of research and industrial projects, this paper explores how the epistemic challenges of accessing subsurface geological formations beneath the sea shape the promises surrounding marine basaltic storage, and how this calls for specific modalities of public regulation and informed community participation.

  • Tony Cabus Kiel University, Germany The Legal Governance of In Situ CO₂ Mineralization Beyond National Jurisdiction

    This paper explores the legal governance challenges that would arise if States pursue CO₂ mineralisation at oceanic ridges in areas beyond national jurisdiction, with particular attention to the Common Heritage of Mankind principle and the implications of the BBNJ Agreement for this emerging technique.

  • Vonintsoa Rafaly WMU-Sasakawa Global Ocean Institute, Sweden Due Diligence in Carbon Storage: An Appraisal of Time in the Law of the Sea

    This paper assesses the temporal scope of due diligence obligations in the law of the sea, exploring how these legal tools apply to century-scale risks from carbon storage activities and addressing the question of how liability and risk management would operate if storage in areas beyond national jurisdiction were to leak decades from now.

  • Cuong Viet Do Vietnam National University, Vietnam Offshore Carbon Storage and Jurisdictional Ambiguities under UNCLOS: A Southeast Asian Perspective

    This paper examines three unresolved challenges — defining the scope of state jurisdiction in overlapping maritime zones, allocating responsibility for transboundary leakage, and reconciling sovereign rights with climate obligations — and proposes a soft-law ASEAN Declaration on Marine CCS Governance as a pioneering regional mechanism.

  • Mohammad Nizam Ashraf Khan and Bhavana Dhoundiyal IILM University, India The Void Below: Bridging the Regulatory Gaps in Sub-Seabed Carbon Storage Beyond National Jurisdiction

    This paper argues that effective governance requires a new multilateral framework harmonising environmental impact assessment procedures, transboundary liability mechanisms, and monitoring protocols, while addressing how the common heritage of mankind principle conflicts with the long-term territorial stability that carbon storage demands.

  • Amanda Wee NUS Centre for International Law, Singapore Implications of Justice on the Evolving Law Governing Carbon Sequestration at Sea

    This paper argues that principles of fairness, intergenerational equity, ecological responsibility, and distributive justice should underpin the legal framework, ensuring that the gaps in the London Convention and Protocol regarding long-term liability, compensation, and protection of the marine environment are addressed through principled deliberation rather than reactive reform.

  • Howard Mwesigwa University of Edinburgh, United Kingdom A Shield or a Double-Edged Sword? An Environmental and Social Justice Appraisal of Offshore CCS

    This paper interrogates whether existing regulatory frameworks adequately address the socio-ecological risks of offshore CCS, examining how projects may externalise environmental burdens to coastal communities, marginalised fishing populations, and future generations while delivering disproportionate benefits to major emitters in the Global North.

  • Mohamed Sobhy Zagazig University, Egypt Transboundary Liability and Environmental Justice in Offshore Carbon Storage: A Comparative Analysis under UNCLOS and the London Protocol

    This paper proposes a tiered transboundary liability model comprising strict operator liability, mandatory financial security, a State-backed residual fund, and harmonised monitoring, complemented by procedural guarantees and distributive safeguards — including benefit-sharing and capacity-building — for affected coastal communities and small-scale fishers.

  • Sumanta Bhattacharya and Bhavneet Kaur Sachdev Asian International University, India Governing CCS at Sea under UNCLOS and EU Law: Harmonizing Transboundary CO₂ Transport, Sub-Seabed Sequestration, Liability Regimes, and Climate Objectives

    This paper examines how UNCLOS and EU legal regimes can converge to address jurisdictional challenges in transboundary CO₂ transport, close gaps between international and EU monitoring and verification provisions, and establish comprehensive liability frameworks balanced between ecological preservation and technological deployment.

  • Ayat-Allah Bouramdane International University of Rabat, Morocco CCS in the Nordic Context: Balancing Climate Ambitions, Environmental Justice, and Socio-Ecological Impacts

    Using a prospective modelling approach combining techno-economic assessments with spatial and social impact evaluations, this paper analyses CCS integration scenarios within Nordic energy systems, finding that while CCS can significantly reduce emissions, the distribution of benefits and risks across communities is uneven.
